%section% Numerals

Elves could count to 12 instead of 10. They actually used both system, but using a base-12 system means the reader has to rewire the way s/he thinks about numbers in Elvish languages. The base-12 system is called the "duodecimal" system.

There is a lot of debate surrounding the duodecimal system, so let's look at some evidence.

Tolkien said that before the Great Journey the elves used their hands as the primitive counting instrument, so they developed a decimal counting system. But later they developed arithmetic and with it, the duodecimal system. Carl Hostetter notes:

Much later (...) the Eldar, leaving behind the primitive beginnings with the hand, devised a counting in sixes and twelves which they used in all more elaborate reckonings; but in daily and colloquial use many of the decimal terms remained in use. (NM/181)

So the decimal system was used for daily counting, and duodecimal for calculations. Apparently a few duodecimal terms made into colloquial speech, like 12 (dozen), 18, and 144 (gross).

However, even though Tolkien mentioned multiple times the duodecimal system, the words for the numbers in Sindarin clearly follow the decimal system. Tolkien didn't give us a lot of material in that topic, so if there are special words for a dozen and eighteen, we don't know them.

It is also implied that the Numenóreans preferred the decimal system, so in practical terms, we should probably use the regular decimal designations of numbers for most texts and works in Sindarin that don't involve arithmetics.

I attempt to approach a reconstruction of duodecimal language in a separate page that will be linked below. The reader is welcome to verify it, but I'd recommend taking it with a pinch of salt.

Cardinal numbers

The decimal system is the same we use on our daily lives. These are the names of the numbers in decimal: 

  0 lad      10 pae
  1 min      11 minib
  2 tâd      12 ýneg        20 taphaen
  3 neledh   13 paenel      30 nelphaen
  4 canad    14 paegan      40 caphaen
  5 leben    15 paeleben    50 lephaen
  6 eneg     16 paeneg      60 enephaen
  7 odog     17 paenodog    70 odophaen
  8 tolodh   18 paedolodh   80 tolophaen     100 toch
  9 neder    19 paeneder    90 nederphaen   1000 meneg

Note that toch, the word for a hundred, is pluralised for higher numbers, so 200 is written "tych §s{dâd|tâd}". The same goes for a thousand: 2000 is pluralised to "menig §s{dâd|tâd}".

Mutation

In larger chains of numbers, don't lenit all of them, only the ones that modify each other. E.g. in nine-hundreds the nine would be lenited.

Writing cardinal numbers

In English, to say the name of a larger number, we break it into thousands, hundreds, tens, and ones. In Sindarin, this is done backwards.

Instead of saying "twenty one", one should say "one and twenty". Instead of "six hundred thirty two", one says "two thirty six hundred".

Ordinal numbers

Ordinal numbers use the suffix -ui. We don't have any evidence of ordinal numbers above 10, but we can make an educated guess. 

  0 --       10 paenui
  1 minui    11 mibui
  2 tadui    12 ýnegui      20 taphaenui
  3 nelui    13 paenelui    30 nelphaenui
  4 canthui  14 paeganthui  40 caphaenui
  5 levnui   15 paelevnui   50 lephaenui
  6 enchui   16 paenchui    60 enephaenui
  7 othui    17 paenothui   70 odophaenui
  8 tolthui  18 paedollui   80 tolophaenui     100 tochui
  9 nedrui   19 paenedrui   90 nederphaenui   1000 mengui

Literature on the subject of Sindarin ordinal numbers is patchy at best. I'll add more information here when I have any.

Word order of numerals

Both cardinal and ordinal numbers tend to come after the nouns in an adjectival position. Examples: 

  beraid **§s{dâd|tâd}**           = "**two** towers"
  andrann **§s{dadui|tadui}**        = "**second** age"
  cyrf **neder** nan edain = "**nine** rings of men" ±lit. "rings nine"±
  cerenyr **§s{chost|host}**        = "**gross** years" (144 years)

Cardinal numbers might be used as nominalised heads of a genitive construct. The meaning is mostly the same, but there might be implied contrasts. See more at the ||#genitive||genitive section||. 

  **neder** cyrf = "**nine** [of] rings" ±(lit.)±

When adjectives are used along with numerals, it's recommended to keep the numeral between the noun and adjectives and not mutate the adjective chain. Also, don't pluralise the numeral. 

  Eight beautiful proud and black horses:
  rych **§s{dolodh|tolodh}** *myrn terleng bain*  = "horses **eight** *black proud beautiful*" ±(lit.)±
